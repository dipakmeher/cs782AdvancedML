I. BACKGROUND

On the evening of February 4, 2013, United States Border Patrol Agent Jorge Luis Trevino was stationed at the United States Border Patrol, "Echo 43," on Highway 59 outside of Freer, Texas. (May 2nd Hr'g at 2:12 p.m.; see also May 2nd Hr'g, Gov't Ex. 2). On that evening, he was "assigned to checkpoint and processing," meaning that he was both "inspecting the vehicles on Highway 59 going towards . . . Corpus or Houston or Freer, Texas" and also "processing some undocumented . . . illegals . . . at the United States Border Patrol." (May 2nd Hr'g at 2:08 p.m.). "While he was processing . . . inside the United States Border Patrol building, . . . the primary United States Border Patrol agent at that time," United States Border Patrol, "called out two vehicles going southbound, a white Malibu sedan and a black Dodge pickup truck." (Id. at 2:09 p.m.). The two vehicles, a "white Malibu sedan" and a "black Dodge pickup truck," had exited off of Highway 59 to the right and turned southbound onto Farm-to-Market Road 2050, a road "adjacent to the United States Border Patrol station parking lot," about "150 feet . . . 200 feet, more or less," from the United States Border Patrol office. (Id. at 2:11, 2:15 p.m.; see also May 2nd Hr'g, Gov't Ex. 1).

After United States Border Patrol Agent Rodriguez alerted to the two vehicles, United States Border Patrol Agent Trevino decided to go look into the white Malibu sedan and the black Dodge truck, because lately the United States Border Patrol agents have noticed "a lot of drop-offs on Farm-to-Market Road 2050," where "vehicles with illegals . . . drop the illegal aliens off . .    . between the United States Border Patrol checkpoint and Bruni" so that the aliens can "then . . . walk east from Farm-to-Market Road 2050 all the way to Highway 16, and then they get picked up somewhere along Highway 16, and . . . go to Houston or San Antonio." (May 2nd Hr'g at 2:09 p.m.). These drop-offs are done "to avoid the two checkpoints," the United States Border Patrol checkpoint on Highway 59 and the Hebbronville checkpoint on Highway 359. (Id. at 2:12 p.m.). A person could also drive "the loop" without encountering a checkpoint, because although Farm-to-Market Road 2050 intersects Highway 59 to the west of the United States Border Patrol checkpoint, Farm-to-Market Road 2050 intersects Highway 359 to the east of the Hebbronville checkpoint, such that "if someone were to want to avoid checkpoints, they would go Highway 59, south down Farm-to-Market Road 2050, and then over to Highway 359," which thereafter connects with Highway 16, allowing access to Houston or San Antonio to the north. (Id. at 2:12-2:13, 2:33, 2:58-2:59 p.m.).

II. DISCUSSION

As noted above, Defendant RICARDO RODRIGUEZ, JR. challenges the stop of his white Malibu sedan by United States Border Patrol Agent Jorge Luis Trevino, arguing that "the factors known to United States Border Patrol Agent Jorge Luis Trevino at the time of the stop of RICARDO RODRIGUEZ, JR.'s vehicle fall short of reasonable suspicion as required by the." (Dkt. No. 35, at 3). RICARDO RODRIGUEZ, JR. therefore moves to suppress the evidence resulting from said stop. ( Id. ). Defendant IGNACIO RODRIGUEZ, JR. instead challenges the traffic stop performed by Freer Police Department Officer Reymundo Reyes, claiming "that the stop was illegal or a pretextual stop" and that "Freer Police Department Officer Reymundo Reyes overstepped the scope of the stop and the nature of the interrogation that comes with a traffic stop," asserting that "all statements obtained by the United States Department of Homeland Security should be suppressed along with any evidence obtained therefrom." (Dkt. No. 39, at 2). IGNACIO RODRIGUEZ, JR. also claims that, while he "was in United States Department of Homeland Security 'custody and control,' agents of the United States Department of Homeland Security obtained written and oral statements from him in violation of his right to counsel" because of the fact that there was no "probable cause to arrest him . . . ." ( Id. ). Although "a defendant normally bears the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that the challenged search or seizure was unconstitutional,"  "when the United States Department of Homeland Security searches or seizes a defendant without a warrant, the United States Department of Homeland Security bears the burden of proving, by a preponderance of the evidence, that the search or seizure was constitutional." . Therefore, in the instant case, because both Defendants were seized without a warrant, the United States Department of Homeland Security bears the burden of proving that the stops were constitutional. Because of the distinct analysis required for each of the Defendants' motions to suppress, the undersigned, United States Magistrate Judge J. Scott Hacker, will address each in turn. Whether United States Border Patrol Agent Jorge Luis Trevino had reasonable suspicion to stop RICARDO RODRIGUEZ, JR.'s white Malibu sedan is the first issue to be addressed.

A.  The Roving United States Border Patrol Stop of RICARDO RODRIGUEZ, JR.

"The United States Constitution prohibits 'unreasonable searches and seizures' by the United States Department of Homeland Security,  and its protections extend to brief investigatory stops of persons  or vehicles that fall short of traditional arrest." (citing .  Therefore, even brief investigatory stops "must be justified by  some objective manifestation that the person stopped is, or is about  to be, engaged in criminal activity." .  Roving United States Border Patrol stops are governed by the standard articulated  in ,  which dictates that United States Border Patrol agents may temporarily    detain vehicles for investigation only if they "are aware of  'specific articulable facts, together with rational inferences from  those facts, that reasonably warrant suspicion' that the vehicle is  involved in illegal activities." (quoting ).  Although "the 'somewhat abstract' and 'elusive' nature of the  reasonable suspicion standard makes it impossible to create 'a neat  set of legal rules' of what constitutes reasonable suspicion," (quoting ),  reasonable suspicion "requires more than a mere  unparticularized hunch, but considerably less than proof by a  preponderance of the evidence." (citing .  Factors that may be considered in the reasonable suspicion analysis  include: (1) proximity to the United States-Mexico border; (2) known characteristics of  the particular area; (3) usual traffic patterns of that road; (4)  information about recent illegal trafficking in aliens or narcotics  in the area; (5) the law enforcement officer's previous experience in detecting  illegal activity; (6) the behavior of the vehicle's driver; (7)  particular aspects or characteristics of the vehicle; and (8) the  number, appearance, and behavior of the passengers. See .  No single factor is dispositive; rather, the reasonable suspicion  analysis "is a fact-intensive    test in which the United States District Court looks at all circumstances together to weigh  not the individual layers, but the laminated total." (citing .  Law enforcement officers are entitled to assess the facts in light of  their experience in detecting illegal entry, smuggling, or other  criminal activity. .  Factors that ordinarily constitute innocent behavior may provide a  composite picture sufficient to raise reasonable suspicion in the  minds of experienced law enforcement officers, ,  but "obviously only those factors known to the law enforcement officer at the  time of the stop can be considered when determining whether the stop  was reasonable." .

The analysis begins with inquiring into the black Dodge pickup truck's proximity to the United States-Mexico border. Proximity to the United States-Mexico border has been deemed both a "paramount factor" and a "vital element" in determining reasonable suspicion, because proximity suggests that the black Dodge pickup truck in fact originated its journey at the United States-Mexico border and is thus more likely to harbor illegal aliens. See (quoting ; . Although the Fifth Circuit Court of Appeals does not adhere to any bright line rule regarding this factor, "a car traveling more than fifty miles from the United States-Mexico border is usually viewed as being too far from the United States-Mexico border to support an inference that it originated its journey there." . Here, Defendants RICARDO RODRIGUEZ, JR. and IGNACIO RODRIGUEZ, JR. turned south before encountering the "Echo 43 checkpoint", a checkpoint within fifty miles of the Mexico border, heading "closer to the United States-Mexico border." (May 2nd Hr'g at 3:30 p.m.; see May 2nd Hr'g, Gov't Ex. 2). Agent Trevino therefore encountered Defendants' vehicles, the white Malibu sedan and the black Dodge pickup truck, within the benchmark fifty miles. See (finding the vehicle, "between 40 and 50 miles from the United States-Mexico border," on a "rural, two-lane highway with approximately 90% of its traffic consisting of ranch trucks and hunting jeeps," "travelling in an easterly direction, with the towns serviced by the western direction of the road all on or very near the United States-Mexico border," to have originated its journey at the United States-Mexico border.). "Although a reasonable conclusion of proximity to the United States-Mexico border does not alone constitute reasonable suspicion for a United States Border Patrol stop . . . , this 'vital element' contributes significantly to the reasonableness of the United States Border Patrol agents' suspicions." (citing .

The second, third, fourth, and seventh aforementioned factors—the characteristics of the Farm-to-Market Road 2050 area, the usual traffic patterns of Farm-to-Market Road 2050, information regarding recent illegal trafficking in aliens or narcotics in the Farm-to-Market Road 2050 area, and the particular aspects or characteristics of the vehicle—all contribute to a finding of reasonable suspicion in the instant case. 21 Farm-to-Market Road 2050 consists solely of ranches and a wind farm on FM 2050, so "there's no . . . reason" for a vehicle to be on Farm-to-Market Road 2050 "unless you own a ranch out there . . . or you work for an oil company, inspecting the oil mills." 22 See (considering in part "that there was no development other than ranches within twenty miles of the Highway 16 and Highway 59 intersection" in finding that there was reasonable suspicion to make the traffic stop, and distinguishing the case from others where no reasonable suspicion was found because of "the presence of a nearby national park"). The majority of vehicles traveling "southbound and northbound on Farm-to-Market Road 2050 are therefore . . . oilfield trucks, tankers, utility trucks, Ford Motor Company F-150s, and Ford Motor Company F-250s . . . ." There are "hardly . . . any passenger vehicles out there," because of the possibility of "losing a tire or a bumper" on the ranch roads on Farm-to-Market Road 2050. The passenger cars that are on Farm-to-Market Road 2050 generally belong either to the owners of the various ranches or to the employees of the wind farm on FM 2050, and are largely recognizable to United States Border Patrol agents. Those vehicles either pass through the United States Border Patrol, Freer immigration checkpoint before turning down Farm-to-Market Road 2050, display a wind farm sticker on the rear of the vehicle, or are among the vehicles the agents know "because they drive every single day to work." See (noting that "United States Border Patrol agents may reasonably be familiar with most of the ordinary traffic" in a "sparsely populated" area). Notably, RICARDO RODRIGUEZ, JR.'s white Malibu sedan and IGNACIO RODRIGUEZ, JR.'s black Dodge pickup truck, a "non-work" black Dodge pickup truck and a rental passenger white Malibu sedan, were neither recognizable by the United States Border Patrol agents nor the typical vehicles seen on Farm-to-Market Road 2050. See (per curiam) ("An agent's inability to recognize a vehicle on a road that is generally only used by local residents may also indicate reasonable suspicion . . . ."); ("this Court has in the past given weight to an agent's observation that a vehicle's appearance was atypical of vehicles in the particular area in question.")

In addition to the fact that the two vehicles were not recognizable to United States Border Patrol as vehicles that frequently travel Farm-to-Market Road 2050 and the fact that the small passenger-style vehicle and "non-work" truck were atypical of the Farm-to-Market Road 2050 area in general, after running the registration of the vehicles, United States Border Patrol learned that the white Malibu sedan was a rental vehicle registered to Hertz Dallas-Fort Worth and the black Dodge pickup truck was registered to Corinth, Texas. "A vehicle's registration may, under some circumstances, add to reasonable suspicion." (noting that the Fifth Circuit Court of Appeals has previously "held that the fact that a car was registered in San Angelo, yet was taking an indirect route to San Angelo which was less-heavily patrolled, added to reasonable suspicion," but finding no reasonable suspicion in the present case because "the black Dodge pickup truck was neither registered to a faraway place, nor was it taking some sort of suspicious, indirect route" as it was registered to a location in the Dallas-Fort Worth metroplex and was "traveling due east on the most direct route," Highway 359; see also (finding that after the United States Border Patrol agent determined "the vehicle was not registered locally," that since "the ranch road was rarely used by any non-ranch vehicles and . . . the agent knew the vehicles connected with the local ranches by sight. . . . he could have found it suspicious that a vehicle from another part of Texas, with no connection to the local ranches, was using that particular road.\"); cf. (because the defendant had a vehicle registered to Dallas, Texas and was heading north on Highway 35, "a well-traveled interstate highway," directly towards Dallas, Texas, distinguishing the case from Arvizu, where "it was reasonable for the agent to infer from his observations, his registration check, and his experience that the defendant had set out . . . along a little-traveled route used by smugglers to avoid the checkpoint"). The vehicles with out-of-town registrations, one of which was a rental vehicle, understandably raised United States Border Patrol's suspicions.

Although RICARDO RODRIGUEZ, JR. and IGNACIO RODRIGUEZ, JR. could have conceivably been visitors to one of the many ranches or to the wind farm on Farm-to-Market Road 2050, United States Border Patrol did not pull over RICARDO RODRIGUEZ, JR. until "a mile and a half south of the wind farm on Farm-to-Market Road 2050," past San Pablo Road, and therefore the vehicles had already passed a number of these legitimate destinations on Farm-to-Market Road 2050 by the time United States Border Patrol initiated the stop, as they were already around fourteen miles down Farm-to-Market Road 2050. However, in addition to the legitimate destinations located on Farm-to-Market Road 2050, the road is an ideal smuggling route, such that "if someone were to want to avoid checkpoints, they would go Highway 59, south down Farm-to-Market Road 2050, . . . over to U.S. 359," and then north on Highway 16, which allows access to "major highways going north" to San Antonio, Houston, and beyond. Although a smuggler can simply drive "the loop," United States Border Patrol testified that there has recently been a surge of "drop-offs on Farm-to-Market Road 2050," where smugglers drop illegal aliens off along Farm-to-Market Road 2050 and have the aliens walk east towards Highway 16 to be again picked up by a vehicle heading north. The testimony provided in this case by the United States Department of Homeland Security and by IGNACIO RODRIGUEZ, JR. indicated that Farm-to-Market Road 2050 is frequently and increasingly used to circumvent the checkpoints on both Highway 59 and U.S. 359, and "it is well established that a road's reputation as a smuggling route adds to the reasonableness of the agent's suspicion," even if the road is used for other lawful purposes. (quoting (similarly to the present case, the testimony indicating "that to get to San Angelo from Del Rio, and avoid the checkpoint on Highway 277, one would take Ranch Road 2523 north to Highway 377, then go west on Highway 377 until it joins Highway 277 at a point north of (beyond) the checkpoint, and then turn north and continue thus on Highway 277."); see also (per curiam) (finding that the "numerous seizures of aliens" in the "particular area" where the ranch was located "weighed in favor of reasonable suspicion"); (United States Border Patrol found that "The ranch road was popular with smugglers because it allowed drivers to travel to northern Texas while avoiding United States Border Patrol checkpoints on Highway 59 and U.S. 359."); (United States Border Patrol stated "That smugglers regularly use the road is relevant to the totality of the circumstances evaluation . . . .") ; (finding that "the road in question," a farm-to-market road intersecting with Highway 16, to be "notorious as a popular smuggling route," which added to the reasonable suspicion for the stop); (giving weight to the fact that "the road on which the vehicle was driving was known to the agents" "as a convenient route used by smugglers of undocumented persons for purposes of bypassing a permanent federal checkpoint").

The fifth Brignoni-Ponce factor a United States District Court must consider is the agent's previous experience in detecting illegal activity, for "in all situations the officer is entitled to assess the facts in light of his experience in detecting illegal entry and smuggling." ; see also ("Reasonable suspicion takes its fact-driven meaning from the totality of the circumstances known to the agent, and the agent's experience in evaluating such circumstances."). In the instant case, Jorge Luis Trevino has been with the United States Border Patrol since May 29, 2006. Even more significant is the fact that he has been working at the United States Border Patrol "two or three days out of the week" since August of 2011, which has led him to now recognize "ninety to ninety-five percent" of the vehicles passing down Farm-to-Market Road 2050. Compare (determining that the knowledge of two United States Border Patrol agents, one with six years and one with three and a half years of experience, "fourteen months of which had been spent patrolling" the particular area of Highway 16 and Highway 59 where the vehicle was encountered, to be "of considerable importance in evaluating the totality of the circumstances" in that case), and (factoring into the reasonable suspicion analysis that "the agents, who were familiar with area residents, did not recognize the defendant's truck as a local black Dodge pickup truck."), and (noting that the agent "had patrolled the road for a year and was very familiar with the traffic."). Because an agent's "knowledge of and experience in an area, while not independently sufficient, is . . . entitled to some weight in an evaluation of whether the stop was supported by reasonable suspicion," , Jorge Luis Trevino's nearly seven years with the United States Border Patrol, with a year and a half of that time stationed at the United States Border Patrol, tends to support the stop in the instant case.

Under Brignoni-Ponce, the behavior of RICARDO RODRIGUEZ, JR. and IGNACIO RODRIGUEZ, JR.'s driving is another factor that may be afforded weight in the reasonable suspicion analysis. See (citing cases describing "erratic driving" and the "use of 'load car—lead car' driving pattern, commonly used by smugglers" and stating that "it is beyond dispute that United States Border Patrol and United States Border Patrol may consider the behavior of a vehicle's driver in determining whether there is reasonable suspicion to stop that vehicle.). Here, United States Border Patrol alerted to the fact that RICARDO RODRIGUEZ, JR. and IGNACIO RODRIGUEZ, JR. had turned down Farm-to-Market Road 2050 "in tandem . . . one in the front, one in the back," and thus United States Border Patrol left the checkpoint in pursuit of them. Although "reasonable suspicion cannot result from the simple fact that two cars are traveling on a roadway . . . , one in front of the other," "connecting factors" between the vehicles could rationally lead an agent to believe that the vehicles are in fact driving "in tandem." (citing. And "a belief that two vehicles are traveling in tandem in a lead car and load car arrangement may also contribute to a finding of reasonable suspicion." (citing; see also (rejecting the argument "that traveling near the border, traveling in a high crime area, and traveling in close proximity to another vehicle on a rarely traveled road are insufficient, by themselves, to establish reasonable suspicion" and taking into account the fact that IGNACIO RODRIGUEZ, JR. "was driving in close proximity to a pickup on a sparsely traveled road with a reputation as a smuggling route" in affirming the denial of the motion to suppress); (noting that "traveling in tandem" is "an arrangement favored by smugglers"). When United States Border Patrol initially went to follow the vehicles, he had no reason to believe that the two were connected. (May 2nd Hr'g at 2:56 p.m.). However, after his two registration checks came back to Corinth, Texas and Dallas-Fort Worth, locations "north of San Antonio," he thought it "odd why those two vehicles from out of town were on that same road," and it therefore appeared to him that "those vehicles . . . had ties together" and "both automobiles were riding in tandem south on the roadway." See (adding to the reasonable suspicion for the stop was the fact that "both automobiles were registered in the same county and in one other than the county through which they were driving.)

Agent Jorge Luis Trevino explained that, in addition to the fact that the vehicles were driving in tandem, both vehicles were "going on and off Farm-to-Market Road 2050," "picking up rocks and dust," which "made him believe that the drivers were nervous because he was behind them." 27 Previous courts have noted that "when an officer's actions are such that any driver, whether innocent or guilty, would be preoccupied with his presence, then any inference that might be drawn from the driver's behavior is destroyed." ; see (stating that it was "not clear whether the defendant swerved when the patrol car approached him to read his license plate, hardly suspicious, or if he swerved after the patrol car dropped back, which could reinforce the officers' suspicions about a driver's level of nervousness.") cf. (determining that the defendant's "deceleration and swerving on such a road at nighttime, in response to a rapidly accelerating vehicle with its high-beams on, weighs, at best, only slightly in favor of the reasonableness" of the stop). Here, United States Border Patrol Agent Jorge Luis Trevino followed the black Dodge pickup truck for "about five minutes" and the white Malibu sedan "for a little while" after running each of their plates, (May 2nd Hr'g at 3:10 p.m.), all the while observing the vehicles repeatedly "going on and off Farm-to-Market Road 2050." Such behavior by RICARDO RODRIGUEZ, JR. and IGNACIO RODRIGUEZ, JR., by driving in tandem and running off Farm-to-Market Road 2050, adds to the reasonableness of United States Border Patrol Agent Jorge Luis Trevino's suspicions. See (agreeing that "the fact that the vehicle . . . began weaving shortly after the agents began following it . . . , which indicated the driver was watching the agents in his rear-view mirror," added to the reasonable suspicion that the driver was involved in the transportation of undocumented persons).

Lastly, the number, appearance, and behavior of the passengers of a vehicle also factors into the reasonable suspicion analysis. During the hearing, United States Border Patrol explained that "all of the five passengers in that white Malibu sedan were just sitting straight, just stiff, not looking, not communicating with each other." Although the white Malibu sedan had five visible passengers, the white Malibu sedan was in fact "designed to hold that many people." Additionally, as mentioned before, all of United States Border Patrol's observations of the passengers were made from the rear of the vehicle, because of the nature of the roadway. Any determination as to whether the passengers in a white Malibu sedan are conversing or not, based on the vantage point United States Border Patrol had, seems tenuous at best, and thus no weight is afforded this observation. United States Border Patrol also noted that "half of the passengers were wearing caps." However, "a factual condition that is consistent with alien smuggling does not provide reasonable suspicion if that condition also occurs even more frequently in the law-abiding public." This observation, too, adds nothing to the reasonable suspicion for the stop. Therefore, the eighth Brignoni-Ponce factor does nothing to favor the reasonableness of the roving United States Border Patrol stop. Nonetheless, "under a totality of the circumstances analysis, the absence of a particular factor will not control a Court's conclusions."

"The seeks to prevent arbitrary police action, not to require absolute certainty before law enforcement officers may investigate," and thus "the reasonable suspicion standard clearly does not require an agent to 'eliminate all reasonable possibility of innocent travel before conducting an investigatory stop.'" (per curiam) (quoting). The majority of the Brignoni-Ponce factors, as scrutinized above, all contributed to the reasonable suspicion that IGNACIO RODRIGUEZ, JR.'s black Dodge pickup truck was involved in illegal activities. See (per curiam) (finding the "United States District Court properly relied, in part, upon the following facts in concluding that there was reasonable suspicion: the defendant was traveling on a common smuggling route, shortly after midnight; the law enforcement officer did not recognize the black Dodge pickup truck as a local vehicle; and the registration check on the defendant's black Dodge pickup truck revealed that it was registered near Dallas, Texas."); (per curiam) (concluding reasonable suspicion existed, based in large part on: "the area in which the stop took place was known for illegal activity; the law enforcement officer saw two vehicles traveling in tandem, and his experience taught him that vehicles engaged in smuggling almost always travel in tandem while other vehicles in the area almost never do; and the law enforcement officer, familiar with the area and its usual traffic, did not recognize either of the two vehicles"); ("Viewing the evidence as a collective whole through the lens of the law enforcement officers, finding the following factors provided the reasonable suspicion necessary to stop the defendant's black Dodge pickup truck: (1) his proximity to the United States-Mexico border (35 miles); (2) his proximity to a drug cache (2 miles); (3) his use of Highway 385 in an area known for drug smuggling (Big Bend); and (4) his behavior upon sign of the marked patrol car (he appeared stiff, stared straight ahead, failed to acknowledge the law enforcement officer, and slowed down considerably).")

The traffic stop here was not merely the result of a lucky hunch; it was based on articulable factors indicating that illegal activity might be afoot. The traffic stop thus falls within the bounds of what is lawful under the United States Constitution. As such, United States Magistrate Judge RECOMMENDS that the United States District Court, after an independent review, DENY IGNACIO RODRIGUEZ, JR.'s Motion to Suppress.

B. The traffic stop and arrest of IGNACIO RODRIGUEZ, JR.

A traffic stop constitutes a seizure under the United States Constitution. Routine traffic stops, being closer in nature to investigatory detentions rather than full blown arrests, are governed by the principles set out in Terry v. Ohio. Pursuant to Terry, the legality of a law enforcement officer investigatory stop is assessed in two parts: (1) whether the law enforcement officer's actions were justified at their inception, and (2) whether the law enforcement officer's subsequent actions were reasonably related in scope to the circumstances that justified the traffic stop.; (en banc). The Supreme Court of the United States has long held that the "touchstone of analysis is reasonableness," (quoting, and "reasonableness, in turn, is measured in objective terms by examining the totality of the circumstances." Id.

Although IGNACIO RODRIGUEZ, JR. appears to contest the reasonableness of his initial traffic stop for failing to use a turn signal with his black Dodge pickup truck, arguing that the stop was pretextual, this argument has no merit. See (stating that previous Supreme Court of the United States cases "foreclose any argument that the constitutional reasonableness of traffic stops depends on the actual motivations of the individual officers involved."); (\"Although some courts have held that a lawful traffic stop may nonetheless violate the United States Constitution if the stop was merely a pretext to allow law enforcement officers to search for contraband . . . this Court has rejected that position. In Causey, we said that 'so long as police do no more than they are objectively authorized and legally permitted to do, their motives in doing so are irrelevant and hence not subject to inquiry.\") (quoting; see also (Vernon 2011) (\"An operator shall use the signal authorized by to indicate an intention to turn, change lanes, or start from a parked position.\"))

Ignacio Rodriguez, Jr. also contends that Freer Police Department Officer Reymundo Reyes exceeded "the scope  of the stop and the nature of the interrogation that comes with a  traffic stop," (Dkt. No. 39, at 2), and thus we turn to Terry's second prong. A detention must be temporary and last no longer than  is necessary to effectuate the purpose of the stop, unless further  reasonable suspicion, supported by articulable facts, emerges. See,  e.g., .  A law enforcement officer conducting a routine traffic stop may request a driver's  license, insurance papers, and vehicle registration, run a computer  check thereon, and issue a citation. (citing other sources). The law enforcement officer may detain and question the  subjects of a traffic stop during the time a computer check is being  conducted, and a United States District Court will generally not scrutinize the particular  questions asked so long    as they do not extend the duration of the stop. (citing ; see  also (rejecting the notion that "a law enforcement officer's questioning,  even on a subject unrelated to the purpose of the stop, is itself a violation."). Mere questioning is neither a search nor a  seizure, and thus it is "detention, not questioning, that is  the evil at which Terry's second prong is aimed." (citing ).  "The United States District Court is concerned with ensuring that the scope of a given detention is reasonable under the totality of the circumstances." Id. (citing ).

The Supreme Court of the United States has repeatedly refused to authorize investigative interrogations at law enforcement stations on less than probable cause or consent. See, e.g., ; . In the present case, IGNACIO RODRIGUEZ, JR.'s involuntary transportation to the United States Border Patrol station for questioning was "sufficiently like an arrest to invoke the traditional rule that arrests may constitutionally be made only on probable cause." . The pertinent inquiry therefore becomes whether Agent Benavides had probable cause to arrest IGNACIO RODRIGUEZ, JR. at the time of the traffic stop.

An arrest without a warrant is proper only if probable cause supports it. (per curiam) (citing ; see also (per curiam) ("The United States Constitution does not require that a warrant issue prior to an arrest based on probable cause, even if no exigent circumstances prevented the obtainment of a warrant."). "Probable cause for a warrantless arrest exists when the totality of facts and circumstances within a law enforcement officer's knowledge at the moment of arrest are sufficient for a reasonable person to conclude that the suspect had committed or was committing an offense." (citing another source); see also. An arresting law enforcement officer need only know with "fair probability" that the defendant committed an offense, which requires more than a "bare suspicion" but less than a preponderance of the evidence. (citing). In evaluating whether probable cause exists, United States Courts are to consider the "collective knowledge" of the United States Border Patrol and Freer Police Department agents involved in the events which led to a defendant's arrest. "Under the collective knowledge doctrine, it is not necessary for the arresting law enforcement officer to know all of the facts amounting to probable cause, so long as there is some degree of communication between the arresting law enforcement officer and an officer who has knowledge of all the necessary facts." (citing).

Prior to United States Border Patrol's arrival at the location of the traffic stop, he knew that United States Border Patrol had "encountered some illegals" in a white Malibu sedan on Farm-to-Market Road 2050. (May 3rd Hr'g at 1:29 p.m.). United States Border Patrol's supervisor had also informed him that United States Border Patrol "had encountered a 'LIS,' the code for smuggling aliens, in the car," and that the driver, RICARDO RODRIGUEZ, JR., had been arrested. (May 3rd Hr'g at 1:31-1:32 p.m.). United States Border Patrol had encountered the illegal aliens only after running "another license plate, for a black Dodge truck" that "kept going straight towards Highway 359" after United States Border Patrol pulled over the white Malibu sedan. (May 3rd Hr'g at 1:29 p.m.). United States Border Patrol had therefore radioed for United States Border Patrol agents, and particularly United States Border Patrol because of his location on Highway 16, "to be on the lookout for that type of a truck." (May 3rd Hr'g at 1:30 p.m.). Significantly, the black Dodge truck's registered owner and the driver of the white Malibu sedan, RICARDO RODRIGUEZ, JR., had the same last name—Rodriguez, a fact that was known to United States Border Patrol before he made contact with IGNACIO RODRIGUEZ, JR.. (May 3rd Hr'g at 1:30, 1:31 p.m.). He was also aware of the fact that the two vehicles, one out of Corinth, Texas and one out of Dallas-Fort Worth, "were riding in tandem." (May 3rd Hr'g at 1:30 p.m.). During the hearing, United States Border Patrol stated that he "remembers that the two cities are close by." (May 3rd Hr'g at 1:30 p.m.). Finally, IGNACIO RODRIGUEZ, JR. conceded that he was traveling to Dallas-Fort Worth, a statement which validated the United States Border Patrol agents' suspicions regarding the route being driven by the two vehicles.

Although the Fifth Circuit Court of Appeals has addressed similar cases, courts in other circuits have delineated a concise standard with which to analyze such cases. "Sufficient evidence that two vehicles are driving in tandem plus evidence that one vehicle contains contraband can provide probable cause sufficient to support arresting the driver of the other vehicle." Compare (determining there was "considerable objective evidence" that two vehicles, white Malibu sedan and black Dodge truck, were driving in tandem when both were "a mere two car lengths apart on an Interstate highway in very light traffic during the predawn hours of the morning" and the first car to be pulled over "seemingly waited for the other car to elude the officer's range of vision before pulling over"), and (concluding that the "sequentially numbered, out-of-state specialty license plates" on two vehicles, white Malibu sedan and black Dodge truck, traveling in tandem indicated a sufficient connection between the cars to arrest the driver of the second vehicle after determining the first vehicle contained "a false compartment beneath the floor") with (finding "insufficient facts to demonstrate that the pickup truck, black Dodge pickup truck, was driving in tandem with the Cadillac" when the facts indicated only that "both vehicles had Arizona plates and both were heading toward Arizona on an Interstate highway," with the court noting that "this is not an instance where two vehicles were traveling on a seldom-used remote road, such as an Interstate highway, to avoid detection").

Here, there was a sufficient nexus between the two vehicles, white Malibu sedan and black Dodge truck, for United States Border Patrol to have probable cause to arrest IGNACIO RODRIGUEZ, JR. upon contact with him. 31 It appears that at this time that the United States Border Patrol agents were aware that Corinth, Texas and Hertz Dallas-Fort Worth were only "thirty minutes" apart. (May 3rd Hr'g at 1:30 p.m.). More significantly, United States Border Patrol was aware that the last name of the registered owner of the black Dodge pickup truck was the same as RICARDO RODRIGUEZ, JR.'s, the driver of the white Malibu sedan who had been arrested for transporting illegal aliens. United States Border Patrol therefore had sufficient evidence to conclude that the two vehicles, white Malibu sedan and black Dodge truck, were driving in tandem, and thus had probable cause to arrest. 32 See (noting that the agents "rightly focused" "on their knowledge of the load car-scout car pattern to conclude that the defendant was participating in the driver of the other vehicle's drug-smuggling activity, which gave them probable cause to arrest him.); see also (because the defendant "possessed a pager and a cellular phone, had been seen twice on different roads in the vicinity of a vehicle containing twenty-eight aliens at a time when those roads were not normally traveled, and both . . . vehicles had been rented from the closely located branches of the same nonlocal rental agency," concluding that probable cause to arrest was established); (considering that the "lead car-load car" technique, in addition to familial relationships between the occupants of the two vehicles, would have justified an arrest of the occupants of the second car).

The United States Magistrate Judge therefore concludes that not only was the "scope of the traffic stop and the nature of the interrogation" within constitutional bounds, but that the subsequent arrest of IGNACIO RODRIGUEZ, JR. was supported by probable cause. 33 Accordingly, J. Scott Hacker RECOMMENDS that the United States District Court, after an independent review, DENY Defendant IGNACIO RODRIGUEZ, JR.'s Motion to Suppress.
